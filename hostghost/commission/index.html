Comissioning a plan of a plan

· Aoife Hayes

a plan of a plan is an unfinished artwork by Lily O’Shea that is based on the structure of a staircase. It is accompanied by a text titled life in a project: a time of no time which explores the relationship between project work and precarity. The research process involved in this work focuses on the pressures surrounding creative pursuit and the issue of affording the time and space to maintain a sustainable art practice.

Commission Process

The commissioning of a plan of a plan can be split into two processes – curating and the curatorial. While these are separate processes, their intersection is inevitable.

a plan of a plan’s production timeline was as follows:

  • Issuing of brief: 30th January 2022
  • First Meeting: 6th February 2022
  • Check-ins: as required
  • Installation: 7th April 2022
  • Artists’ talk: 9th April 2022
  • Uninstall: 9th April 2022 (evening)

This timeline was developed after the initial meeting with Lily O’Shea. At this point the brief had been issued and potential concepts were being explored and ideas beginning to germinate. Evidently, this is a sparse production timeline that represents a bureaucratic step.

The casual nature of this timeline is reflective of my curatorial choice to withdraw. I decided that I did not want to dictate the parameters of the artist’s work schedule or set deadlines within this period. This is not to say that I was removed from the process as curator – naturally, I was available and on hand throughout the process to offer any support or advice required.

Reflecting now, the vagueness of this production timeline succeeded in giving complete agency to the artist over their time and labour. I understand now that the act of curating is not simply an act of bureaucracy – but compassionate bureaucracy.

Commission Brief

Below is the issued commission brief that resulted in the construction of *a plan of a plan. The brief invited Lily to take the title host/ghost and below notes on approach as a starting point and encouraged personal and reflective interpretation.

The commission brief was primarily concerned with:

  • The experience of belonging and identity in Cork city and the distortion of this experience through a culture of neglect.
  • The urban environment – characterised by misused space, difficulty in sustaining creative practice and resulting curtailed ‘productivity’.
  • The pandemic’s exacerbation of our contemporary state of precarity.
  • The construction of new identities of place without commodifying current identities.
  • A process-led practice and documentation of process through photographic or text reflections

Initial Approach

The proposed approach prioritised process, the local, agency and care and initially valued the experience of this approach over the production of a final project. The approach was rooted in reflective thought, engaged research and caring consideration for the place-making process. An emphasis was placed on everyday meditations – demonstrating that a socially conscious practice need not be abruptly disruptive or radical in its active critique. The commission encouraged documentation from an early stage of production in the form of journaling, voice-note musings/notes app rants, scribbles, doodles etc.

It was the intention that this approach would remove host/ghost from the realm of performativity and contribute to constructive place-making, while also affording those involved a sense of belonging.

Executed Approach

It quickly became clear that the proposed approach was not sustainable within the production timeline and did not lend itself to constructive place-making, slowness or situatedness. By advocating slowness, I risked reproducing the conditions I sought to dismantle. I learned here that identifying a sustainable curatorial approach is not a choice between fast and slow but demands respect for the variety of individual experiences within lived time.

Now, I understand the role of curator as that of a timekeeper, involving the negotiation of both my time and the time of others. It was my initial intention that the commission would produce new work in direct response to a space, however this was not possible as the venue was secured late in the process. This restriction inspired a shift within my research interests from concepts of place-making and identity towards issues of temporalities, labour and chronopolitics.

Following this revelation, accelerating towards the installation date took precedence and the idealised vision of luxuriating in the process was somewhat abandoned. The approach transformed into one that practiced refusal, subversion and withdrawal rather than slowness.

This new approach gave agency to both Lily and myself over our time and labour, allowing us to determine the parameters of our daily rest and work.

As a result, a plan of a plan became the anchor of this research and continues to inform my practice. The staircase structure is something real and tangible and demonstrates a sustainable framework for artistic production.

Once a plan of a plan was assembled in situ, the upstairs mezzanine felt grounded – this was the missing piece.
